Student’s t-Distribution#

  1. When to Use t-Distribution

    • In Z-tests, we require the population standard deviation (σ).

    • Often, σ is unknown, which makes Z-tests inapplicable.

    • In such cases, we use Student’s t-distribution with the sample standard deviation (s).

  2. t-Test Formula

    \[ t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{s / \sqrt{n}} \]

    • \(\bar{X}\) = sample mean

    • μ = population mean (hypothesized)

    • s = sample standard deviation

    • n = sample size

  3. Degree of Freedom (DOF)

    • Important parameter for t-distribution.

    • Formula:

      \[ \text{DOF} = n - 1 \]

    • Example: For 3 people and 3 chairs, only 2 people have options → DOF = 2.

  4. t-Table

    • Similar to Z-table but depends on DOF.

    • Provides critical values for one-tailed or two-tailed tests based on confidence levels.

    • Used to determine if we reject or fail to reject the null hypothesis.

  5. Key Points

    • t-distribution is used when population standard deviation is unknown.

    • Sample standard deviation replaces population standard deviation.

    • DOF adjusts the shape of the t-distribution.

    • t-table helps in hypothesis testing (one-tailed and two-tailed).

Problem Statement#

A nutritionist claims that a new diet plan helps reduce weight by an average of 5 kg in one month. To verify this, a sample of 10 participants follows the diet plan for a month. At the end of the month, the sample shows an average weight loss of 4.2 kg with a sample standard deviation of 0.8 kg.

Question: At a 5% significance level, can we conclude that the diet plan reduces weight by less than the claimed 5 kg?

Notes:

  • Population standard deviation is unknown.

  • Use Student’s t-test (one-tailed, left-tailed test).

Solution#

Step 1: Set Up Hypotheses#

Since we want to test if the weight loss is less than 5 kg, this is a left-tailed t-test:

\[ H_0: \mu = 5 \quad (\text{Diet plan reduces 5 kg}) \]
\[ H_1: \mu < 5 \quad (\text{Diet plan reduces less than 5 kg}) \]

Step 2: Determine Test Statistic#

The t-test formula:

\[ t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{s / \sqrt{n}} \]

Where:

  • \(\bar{X} = 4.2\) (sample mean)

  • μ = 5 (claimed mean)

  • s = 0.8 (sample standard deviation)

  • n = 10 (sample size)

Step 3: Calculate t-value#

  1. Standard error (SE):

\[ SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} = \frac{0.8}{\sqrt{10}} \approx \frac{0.8}{3.162} \approx 0.253 \]

  1. t-value:

\[ t = \frac{4.2 - 5}{0.253} = \frac{-0.8}{0.253} \approx -3.16 \]

Step 4: Determine Critical t-value#

  • Degrees of Freedom: \(df = n - 1 = 10 - 1 = 9\)

  • Significance level: α = 0.05, left-tailed test

  • From t-table, critical t-value \(t_{critical} \approx -1.833\)

Step 5: Compare t-value with t-critical#

\[ t_{calculated} = -3.16 \quad \text{vs} \quad t_{critical} = -1.833 \]

  • Since t_calculated < t_critical, we reject the null hypothesis.

Step 6: Conclusion#

At 5% significance level, there is enough evidence to conclude that the diet plan reduces weight by less than the claimed 5 kg.

Problem Statement#

A team of researchers wants to test a new medication to see if it has an effect on intelligence. The population average IQ is 100. A sample of 30 participants who took the medication has a mean IQ of 140 with a sample standard deviation of 20.

Question: At a 95% confidence level (α = 0.05), can we conclude that the medication affects intelligence?

  • Notes:

    • Population standard deviation is unknown → use t-test

    • Effect could be positive or negative → two-tailed test

Solution Summary#

  1. Hypotheses

    • \(H_0: \mu = 100\) (no effect)

    • \(H_1: \mu \neq 100\) (medication has an effect)

  2. Parameters

    • Sample size: n = 30

    • Sample mean: \(\bar{X} = 140\)

    • Sample standard deviation: s = 20

    • Degrees of Freedom: \(df = n - 1 = 29\)

    • Confidence level: 95% → α = 0.05

  3. Critical t-value (two-tailed)

    • From t-table for df = 29, α = 0.05 → \(t_{critical} = ±2.045\)

  4. Test Statistic Calculation

\[ t = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{s / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{140 - 100}{20 / \sqrt{30}} \approx 10.96 \]

  1. Decision Rule

    • Reject \(H_0\) if \(t < -2.045\) or \(t > 2.045\)

  2. Decision

    • t_calculated = 10.96 > 2.045 → Reject H0

  3. Conclusion

    • The medication has significantly affected intelligence.

    • Since t > 0, the effect is an increase in IQ.

Key Takeaways

  • Use t-test when population standard deviation is unknown.

  • Degrees of freedom affect the t-distribution.

  • Compare t-statistic with t-critical values to decide whether to reject H0.

  • Two-tailed test checks for both increase and decrease in effect.

Decision Guide: t-Test vs Z-Test#

  • Do you know the population standard deviation (σ)?

    • No

      • Use t-test

    • Yes

      • Is sample size n > 30?

        • Yes

          • Use Z-test

        • No

          • Use t-test
