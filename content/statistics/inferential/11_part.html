Central Limit Theorem (CLT)#

Definition#

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that:

When we take repeated random samples from a population and calculate their means, the distribution of these sample means will approximate a normal distribution (bell curve) ‚Äî regardless of the shape of the original population distribution ‚Äî if the sample size is large enough (n ‚â• 30 is a common rule of thumb).

Key Points#

  1. Works even if the population distribution is not normal (e.g., skewed, uniform).

  2. The larger the sample size (n) ‚Üí the closer the sample mean distribution is to a normal distribution.

  3. The mean of the sample means = the population mean (Œº).

  4. The spread of sample means is smaller than population spread, and is given by the Standard Error (SE):

\[ SE = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

where

  • \(\sigma\) = population standard deviation

  • \(n\) = sample size

Why It‚Äôs Important?#

  • It allows us to use normal distribution methods (Z-test, t-test, ANOVA) even when the population is not normal.

  • It is the foundation of inferential statistics ‚Üí helps us make conclusions about populations from samples.

Example#

  • Suppose exam scores in a college are right-skewed (most students score low, a few very high).

  • If we randomly take small samples (say 5 students each), their average will still look skewed.

  • But if we take many larger samples (say n = 50 students each) and plot the means ‚Üí the distribution of those means will look normal (bell curve).
