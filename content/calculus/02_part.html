Integration Calculus#

Integration is the process of adding up infinitely small pieces to find the whole. It is often described as the reverse of differentiation.

What Integration Tells Us#

  1. Area under a curve If you have a function \(f(x)\), the integral

    \[ \int f(x)\,dx \]

    gives the total area under the curve between two points.

  2. Accumulation It measures total change when you know the rate of change.

    • Example: If velocity \(v(t)\) is given, then

      \[ \int v(t)\,dt = \text{distance traveled} \]

Types of Integrals#

  1. Indefinite Integral

    • No limits of integration.

    • Represents a family of functions (plus a constant \(C\)).

    • Example:

      \[ \int 2x\,dx = x^2 + C \]

  2. Definite Integral

    • With limits (from \(a\) to \(b\)).

    • Represents a specific numerical value (area/total).

    • Example:

      \[ \int_{0}^{2} 2x\,dx = [x^2]_{0}^{2} = 4 \]

Why Integration is Important#

  • Physics ‚Üí distance, work, energy.

  • Economics ‚Üí total cost, profit, revenue.

  • Probability ‚Üí finding probabilities using curves.

  • Engineering & Data Science ‚Üí optimization, continuous models.

In short: Integration = Summing up small parts to get the whole (area, volume, total change).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Define function
x = np.linspace(0, 2, 200)
y = 2 * x

# Plot function
plt.figure(figsize=(8,5))
plt.plot(x, y, label=r"$f(x) = 2x$", color="blue")

# Shade area under the curve from 0 to 2
x_fill = np.linspace(0, 2, 200)
y_fill = 2 * x_fill
plt.fill_between(x_fill, y_fill, alpha=0.3, color="skyblue", label="Area = ‚à´ 2x dx from 0 to 2")

# Labels and title
plt.title("Integration: Area Under the Curve", fontsize=14)
plt.xlabel("x")
plt.ylabel("f(x)")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)

plt.show()
