üéØ Hypothesis Testing

üîç What is Hypothesis Testing?#

Hypothesis Testing is a core concept of inferential statistics. It helps us draw conclusions about a population based on sample data.

Goal: Use a sample to test assumptions (hypotheses) about a population parameter (like mean, proportion, etc.).

üß† Why Do We Use It?#

In many real-world cases, we can‚Äôt measure the entire population. So we use sample data to make an informed judgment about the population.

üìã Steps in Hypothesis Testing#

1. State the Null Hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ)#

  • This is the default assumption (nothing unusual is happening).

  • Example: ‚ÄúThe person is not guilty.‚Äù or ‚ÄúThe average pass percentage is 85%.‚Äù

2. State the Alternate Hypothesis (H‚ÇÅ or Ha)#

  • This is what you‚Äôre trying to prove (opposite of H‚ÇÄ).

  • Example: ‚ÄúThe person is guilty.‚Äù or ‚ÄúThe average pass percentage is not 85%.‚Äù

3. Perform Statistical Analysis#

  • Collect and analyze data.

  • Techniques include: Z-test, T-test, Chi-square test, ANOVA, F-test, etc.

  • In real-world terms: like collecting evidence (e.g., DNA, fingerprints in a courtroom).

4. Make a Decision#

  • Based on the result, either:

    • Reject the null hypothesis, or

    • Fail to reject the null hypothesis (we don‚Äôt prove H‚ÇÄ; we just don‚Äôt have enough evidence against it).

üß™ Example Scenario#

Problem: A college in District A claims an average pass percentage of 85%. A new college has a sample of 100 students with a 90% pass rate and 4% standard deviation.

Question: Does this new college have a different pass percentage?

üßæ Hypotheses:#

  • H‚ÇÄ (Null): Œº = 85 (No difference in pass percentage)

  • H‚ÇÅ (Alternate): Œº ‚â† 85 (Pass percentage is different)

üîé Since the question is asking about ‚Äúa different‚Äù percentage (not specifically greater or less), it is a two-tailed test.

To complete the hypothesis testing, you‚Äôll learn:

  • How to compute Z-scores or T-scores

  • Understand p-values and significance levels (Œ±)

  • How to interpret the results statistically

All of this will be covered in the next videos in the series.

üî∑ What is Confidence Interval (CI)?#

A confidence interval gives you a range of values that is likely to contain the true population parameter (like mean or proportion) with a certain level of confidence.

‚úÖ Definition:#

A confidence interval is an estimated range of values that is likely to include an unknown population parameter, calculated from a given set of sample data.

üìå Example:#

Let‚Äôs say you survey 100 people and calculate the sample mean height as 170 cm. You also compute a 95% confidence interval as:

[168 cm, 172 cm]

This means:

‚ÄúWe are 95% confident that the true average height of the entire population lies between 168 and 172 cm.‚Äù

üß† Key Points:#

  • CI = Point Estimate ¬± Margin of Error

  • Common confidence levels: 90%, 95%, 99%

  • A wider CI gives more certainty but less precision.

  • Narrower CI gives more precision but less confidence.

üî∂ What is Significance Value (Œ±)?#

The significance value (denoted by Œ±) is the threshold used to decide whether to reject the null hypothesis in hypothesis testing.

‚úÖ Definition:#

The significance value is the maximum probability of making a Type I error (i.e., rejecting the null hypothesis when it‚Äôs actually true) that you‚Äôre willing to tolerate.

üí° Common Choices:#

  • Œ± = 0.05 ‚Üí 95% confidence

  • Œ± = 0.01 ‚Üí 99% confidence

  • Œ± = 0.10 ‚Üí 90% confidence

üß† Key Point:#

  • If your p-value < Œ±, you reject the null hypothesis.

  • If your p-value ‚â• Œ±, you fail to reject the null hypothesis.

üìä Relationship Between Confidence Interval and Significance Level#

  • Confidence Level = 1 ‚àí Significance Level

So, a 95% confidence interval corresponds to a significance level Œ± = 0.05.

üß™ Real-Life Analogy:#

Let‚Äôs say you‚Äôre testing a new medicine.

  • CI tells you: ‚ÄúWe are 95% confident the true improvement rate lies between 60% and 70%.‚Äù

  • Œ± = 0.05 tells you: ‚ÄúWe‚Äôll only believe the medicine works if the probability of this result happening by chance is less than 5%.‚Äù

üéØ Summary: Understanding p-value in Hypothesis Testing#

üîç What is a p-value?#

  • A p-value is a number calculated from a statistical test that tells us:

    ‚ÄúHow likely is it to get the observed data (or something more extreme) if the null hypothesis is true?‚Äù

  • It‚Äôs a measure of evidence against the null hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ).

üß† Real-Life Analogy (Keyboard Example)#

Imagine where you press the spacebar on a keyboard:

  • Most touches are in the center ‚Üí frequent (high p-value)

  • Rare touches on the corner ‚Üí less frequent (low p-value)

üü¢ p = 0.2 ‚Üí 20 touches out of 100 ‚Üí relatively common üî¥ p = 0.01 ‚Üí 1 touch out of 100 ‚Üí rare event

‚öñÔ∏è Hypothesis Testing Recap#

  1. Null Hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ): Default assumption (e.g., coin is fair)

  2. Alternate Hypothesis (H‚ÇÅ): Opposite assumption (e.g., coin is not fair)

  3. Statistical Test (e.g., Z-test, T-test): Produces a p-value

üß™ Example: Fair Coin Toss#

  • Tossed 100 times

  • Expected: 50 Heads, 50 Tails

  • Observed: 70 Heads

Step 1: Set Hypotheses#

  • H‚ÇÄ: Coin is fair

  • H‚ÇÅ: Coin is not fair

Step 2: Define Significance Level (Œ±)#

  • Typical value: Œ± = 0.05

  • This gives us a 95% confidence interval

  • So, only 2.5% at each tail is considered the rejection region

Step 3: Compare p-value with Œ±#

‚úÖ Final Takeaways#

  • Low p-value (e.g., 0.01): Strong evidence against H‚ÇÄ ‚Üí reject

  • High p-value (e.g., 0.3): Not enough evidence ‚Üí fail to reject

  • P-value helps decide whether an observed result is statistically significant

Bell Curve with Rejection Regions#

  • X-axis: Number of Heads (0 to 100)

  • Mean at 50

  • Shaded region in the middle: Confidence interval (e.g., 30‚Äì70)

  • Outer shaded tails: Rejection region (Œ± = 0.05 ‚Üí 2.5% each side)

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import norm

# Parameters for the normal distribution
mu = 50       # Mean (expected number of heads)
sigma = 10    # Standard deviation
alpha = 0.05  # Significance level

# X range for plotting
x = np.linspace(mu - 4*sigma, mu + 4*sigma, 1000)
y = norm.pdf(x, mu, sigma)

# Calculate the critical values for 2.5% on each tail
z_critical = norm.ppf(1 - alpha/2)
left_crit = mu - z_critical * sigma
right_crit = mu + z_critical * sigma

# Plot the normal distribution
plt.figure(figsize=(12, 6))
plt.plot(x, y, label='Normal Distribution (Œº=50, œÉ=10)', color='blue')

# Fill the rejection regions
plt.fill_between(x, y, where=(x < left_crit), color='red', alpha=0.5, label='Rejection Region (Left)')
plt.fill_between(x, y, where=(x > right_crit), color='red', alpha=0.5, label='Rejection Region (Right)')

# Fill the acceptance region
plt.fill_between(x, y, where=(x >= left_crit) & (x <= right_crit), color='green', alpha=0.3, label='Acceptance Region (95% CI)')

# Mark an example p-value point
example_x = 70
example_p = norm.sf(example_x, mu, sigma) * 2  # Two-tailed
plt.axvline(example_x, color='black', linestyle='--', linewidth=2, label=f'Observed = 70 (p ‚âà {example_p:.3f})')

# Labels and legend
plt.title("Hypothesis Testing: p-value and Rejection Region")
plt.xlabel("Number of Heads in 100 Tosses")
plt.ylabel("Probability Density")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

üéØ Statistical Hypothesis Testing using Z-Test#

‚úÖ Overview:#

As part of inferential statistics, we perform hypothesis testing to draw conclusions about a population using sample data. One such test is the Z-test.

üìå Types of Statistical Tests Introduced:#

  • Z-Test: For large sample sizes (n ‚â• 30) and known population standard deviation.

  • T-Test: When population standard deviation is unknown or sample size is small (< 30).

  • Chi-Square Test: For categorical data.

  • ANOVA Test: For comparing variances among groups.

üß™ When to Use a Z-Test?#

Use a Z-Test when:

  1. Population standard deviation is known

  2. Sample size n ‚â• 30

üìù Problem Statement:#

The population mean height is 168 cm with a standard deviation of 3.9 cm. A doctor takes a sample of 36 individuals and finds their mean height = 169.5 cm. At a 95% confidence level, determine whether the sample provides enough evidence to reject the claim that the population mean is 168 cm.

üîÅ Step-by-Step Hypothesis Testing:#

1. Set Hypotheses:#

  • Null Hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ): Œº = 168 cm (population mean is 168 cm)

  • Alternate Hypothesis (H‚ÇÅ): Œº ‚â† 168 cm (mean is not 168 cm) ‚Üí two-tailed test

2. Significance Level (Œ±):#

  • CI = 95% ‚Üí Œ± = 1 ‚àí 0.95 = 0.05

  • Since it‚Äôs a two-tailed test:

    • Left tail: 0.025

    • Right tail: 0.025

3. Decision Boundary:#

  • Find critical Z-values for Œ±/2 = 0.025 from Z-table:

    • Z = ¬±1.96

  • So, if computed Z lies outside [-1.96, 1.96], we reject H‚ÇÄ

4. Perform Z-Test:#

We would now use the Z formula:

\[ Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \]
\[ Z = \frac{169.5 - 168}{3.9 / \sqrt{36}} = \frac{1.5}{0.65} ‚âà 2.31 \]

5. Decision:#

  • Computed Z = 2.31

  • Since 2.31 > 1.96, it lies outside the 95% confidence interval

  • ‚úÖ Conclusion: Reject the Null Hypothesis ‚Üí There is enough evidence to suggest the mean is different from 168 cm.

üß† Key Concepts Covered:#

  • Hypothesis structure (H‚ÇÄ vs H‚ÇÅ)

  • Conditions for using Z-Test

  • Confidence Interval and Significance Level (Œ±)

  • Two-tailed testing

  • Z-score calculation and interpretation

import math
from scipy.stats import norm

# Given values
population_mean = 168
population_std = 3.9
sample_size = 36
sample_mean = 169.5
confidence_level = 0.95

# Step 1: Calculate standard error
standard_error = population_std / math.sqrt(sample_size)

# Step 2: Calculate Z-score
z_score = (sample_mean - population_mean) / standard_error

# Step 3: Determine critical Z-value for 95% confidence (two-tailed)
alpha = 1 - confidence_level
z_critical = norm.ppf(1 - alpha / 2)  # two-tailed

# Step 4: Decision
reject_null = abs(z_score) > z_critical

# Print results
print(f"Z-score: {z_score:.2f}")
print(f"Z-critical (two-tailed): ¬±{z_critical:.2f}")
print("Result:", "Reject null hypothesis" if reject_null else "Fail to reject null hypothesis")

Z-critical (two-tailed): ¬±1.96
Result: Reject null hypothesis

üìä Student‚Äôs t-Distribution#

In this session, we discussed the Student‚Äôs t-distribution and when to use it compared to the z-distribution.

‚úÖ When to Use z-distribution#

  • Used when the population standard deviation (œÉ) is known

  • Formula:

    \[ Z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \]

üö´ The Problem with z-distribution#

  • In many real-world scenarios, we do not know the population standard deviation

  • It‚Äôs often impossible or impractical to calculate since we don‚Äôt have data for the entire population

‚úÖ When to Use Student‚Äôs t-distribution#

  • Use t-distribution when œÉ is unknown

  • Replace œÉ with the sample standard deviation (s)

  • New formula:

    \[ t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{s / \sqrt{n}} \]

üß† Degree of Freedom (df)#

  • Important parameter for the t-distribution

  • Calculated as:

    \[ \text{df} = n - 1 \]

  • Helps identify the correct row in the t-table

Analogy: If 3 people must choose 3 unique chairs, only the first 2 really have choices ‚Üí df = 3 ‚Äì 1 = 2

üìà t-Table Overview#

  • The t-table provides critical values based on:

    • Degrees of freedom

    • Confidence level

    • Whether it‚Äôs a one-tailed or two-tailed test

üìä Summary: One-Sample t-Test Explanation and Example#

üîπ When to Use t-Test vs. z-Test#

  • Use z-test: When population standard deviation (œÉ) is known and n ‚â• 30

  • Use t-test: When œÉ is unknown (we use sample standard deviation instead)

üß™ Problem Statement#

A new medication is tested to see if it affects intelligence. The population IQ mean is 100. A sample of 30 people who took the medication has a mean IQ = 140 and sample std dev = 20.

üìå Step-by-Step Approach#

1. Known Values#

  • Population mean (Œº) = 100

  • Sample mean (\(\bar{x}\)) = 140

  • Sample std dev (s) = 20

  • Sample size (n) = 30

  • Confidence level = 95% ‚Üí Œ± = 0.05

  • Degrees of freedom (df) = \(n - 1 = 29\)

2. Hypotheses#

  • Null hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ): Œº = 100

  • Alternative hypothesis (H‚ÇÅ): Œº ‚â† 100 (‚áí two-tailed test)

3. Critical t-value from t-table#

  • For df = 29 and Œ± = 0.05 (two-tailed)

  • Critical value (¬±t‚ÇÄ.‚ÇÄ‚ÇÇ‚ÇÖ) ‚âà ¬±2.045

4. Compute the Test Statistic#

\[ t = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{s / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{140 - 100}{20 / \sqrt{30}} ‚âà 10.96 \]

5. Decision Rule#

  • If t > 2.045 or t < -2.045, reject H‚ÇÄ

Since t = 10.96 > 2.045, it falls in the rejection region

‚úÖ Conclusion#

  • The null hypothesis is rejected

  • The medication has a statistically significant effect on IQ

  • Since t is positive and far right, the medication increased intelligence

üè∑Ô∏è Test Type:#

One-sample, two-tailed t-test

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import t

# Given values
population_mean = 100
sample_mean = 140
sample_std = 20
sample_size = 30
confidence_level = 0.95
alpha = 1 - confidence_level
df = sample_size - 1

# Calculate t-statistic
standard_error = sample_std / np.sqrt(sample_size)
t_statistic = (sample_mean - population_mean) / standard_error

# Critical t value for two-tailed test
t_critical = t.ppf(1 - alpha/2, df)

# Create x range for t-distribution plot
x = np.linspace(-5, 5, 1000)
y = t.pdf(x, df)

# Plot the t-distribution
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.plot(x, y, label=f't-distribution (df={df})')
plt.fill_between(x, y, where=(x <= -t_critical), color='red', alpha=0.3, label='Rejection Region (left)')
plt.fill_between(x, y, where=(x >= t_critical), color='red', alpha=0.3, label='Rejection Region (right)')
plt.axvline(t_statistic, color='blue', linestyle='--', label=f't-statistic = {t_statistic:.2f}')
plt.axvline(-t_critical, color='black', linestyle=':', label=f'Critical value = ¬±{t_critical:.2f}')
plt.axvline(t_critical, color='black', linestyle=':')

plt.title('One-Sample Two-Tailed t-Test')
plt.xlabel('t-value')
plt.ylabel('Probability Density')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True)
plt.show()

# Return t-statistic and decision
t_statistic, t_critical, abs(t_statistic) > t_critical

(10.954451150103322, 2.045229642132703, True)

üîç Definitions#

  • Type I Error (False Positive)

    • Rejecting H‚ÇÄ when it is actually true

    • Controlled by Œ± (significance level)

  • Type II Error (False Negative)

    • Failing to reject H‚ÇÄ when it is actually false

üß† Real-World Analogy#

Example: Medical Test

Type I Error: Diagnosing someone as sick when they are actually healthy.

Type II Error: Missing a disease in someone who is actually sick.

üîç What is Œ≤ (beta) in Hypothesis Testing?#

  • Œ≤ (beta) is the probability of making a Type II Error.

  • That means: üëâ You fail to reject the null hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ) even though H‚ÇÄ is actually false.

üí° What is Power of a Test?#

  • The power of a statistical test is defined as:

\[ \text{Power} = 1 - \beta \]

  • So, Power = Probability that you correctly reject a false null hypothesis.

üéì Topic: Bayes Statistics & Bayes‚Äô Theorem#

üîπ 1. What is Bayesian Statistics?#

  • An approach to data analysis and parameter estimation using Bayes‚Äô Theorem.

  • Helps in updating probabilities based on new evidence.

üîπ 2. Independent vs Dependent Events#

  • Independent Events: One event does not affect another (e.g., rolling a die, tossing a coin).

  • Dependent Events: One event affects the outcome of another (e.g., drawing marbles without replacement).

üîπ 3. Conditional Probability#

  • Formula:

    \[ P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B|A) \]

  • This is used when events are dependent.

üîπ 4. Bayes‚Äô Theorem#

Derived from conditional probability:

\[ P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)} \]

Also written as:

\[ P(B|A) = \frac{P(A|B) \cdot P(B)}{P(A)} \]

  • A, B: Events

  • P(A|B): Probability of A given B is true

  • P(B|A): Probability of B given A is true

  • P(A), P(B): Individual probabilities (priors)

üîπ 5. Application in Machine Learning (Naive Bayes Classifier)#

Given features:

  • x‚ÇÅ = size of house, x‚ÇÇ = number of rooms, x‚ÇÉ = location

  • y = price (output)

We compute:

\[ P(y | x‚ÇÅ, x‚ÇÇ, x‚ÇÉ) = \frac{P(y) \cdot P(x‚ÇÅ, x‚ÇÇ, x‚ÇÉ | y)}{P(x‚ÇÅ, x‚ÇÇ, x‚ÇÉ)} \]

Used to predict y given known features.

‚úÖ Key Takeaways#

  • Bayes‚Äô Theorem updates probability with new evidence.

  • It‚Äôs foundational for Naive Bayes algorithm in ML.

  • Understanding conditional probability is crucial to grasp Bayes.

üìå Topic: Confidence Interval & Margin of Error#

‚úÖ What is a Confidence Interval (CI)?#

  • A confidence interval gives a range of values likely to contain the true population mean.

  • It‚Äôs calculated using the formula:

    \[ \text{CI} = \text{Point Estimate} \pm \text{Margin of Error} \]

‚úÖ When to Use Z-Test vs T-Test for CI#

  • Use Z-Test when:

    • Population standard deviation (œÉ) is known.

  • Use T-Test when:

    • Population standard deviation is unknown.

    • Sample size is small (n < 30).

‚úÖ Formula (Z-distribution)#

\[ \text{CI} = \bar{x} \pm Z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \]

Where:

  • \(\bar{x}\) = sample mean

  • \(\sigma\) = population standard deviation

  • \(n\) = sample size

  • \(Z_{\alpha/2}\) = z-score for desired confidence level (e.g., 1.96 for 95%)

üß™ Example: CAT Verbal Section Scores#

  • Population standard deviation (œÉ): 100

  • Sample mean (\(\bar{x}\)): 520

  • Sample size (n): 30

  • Confidence Level: 95% (Œ± = 0.05 ‚Üí \(Z_{\alpha/2} = 1.96\))

üìê Margin of Error:#

\[ \text{ME} = 1.96 \cdot \frac{100}{\sqrt{30}} ‚âà 39.2 \]

üìç Confidence Interval:#

\[ \text{Lower Bound} = 520 - 39.2 = 480.8 \text{Upper Bound} = 520 + 39.2 = 559.2 \]

üìä Conclusion:#

‚ÄúWe are 95% confident that the average CAT exam score lies between 480.8 and 559.2.‚Äù

üîÅ Note:#

  • If you use T-distribution, replace \(Z_{\alpha/2}\) with \(T_{\alpha/2, df}\), using degrees of freedom (n-1).
